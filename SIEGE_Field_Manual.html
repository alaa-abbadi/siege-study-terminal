00The Doctrine: Marks Are a System

The student who panics studies harder. The operator studies the exam.

Top marks are not a personality trait or a gift. They are the output of a closed feedback loop: do real questions → see exactly where you bleed marks → repair that precise gap → prove the repair on a fresh paper → repeat until the bleeding stops. SIEGE exists to run that loop faster than you could by hand, and to never let you lie to yourself about where you actually stand.

The five laws everything in this manual obeys

LawWhy it wins marks
1. Active recall > re-readingPulling an answer out of your head (not recognising it on a page) is what the exam tests and what builds durable memory. Every SIEGE mode is built to make you produce, not passively review.
2. Spaced repetitionMemory decays on a curve. Reviewing just before you'd forget resets the curve higher for less effort. The flashcard boxes and the Decay Curve chart are this law made visible.
3. Past papers are the syllabusExaminers reuse question shapes, traps, and mark-scheme wording. Drilling real papers teaches the actual game. SIEGE only ever indexes real CAIE/Edexcel codes — it never invents questions.
4. The mark scheme is the rulebookMarks are awarded for specific words and structures, not vibes. You must learn to think like the marker. MARK SCHEME and COMMAND WORDS modes drill exactly this.
5. Measure or it didn't happenWhat you don't log, you can't fix. Logging every score and every wrong question turns "I think I'm okay at this" into a number you can attack.
The one-sentence strategyConvert every weakness into a logged number, let the Analytics terminal rank those numbers by yield, and spend tomorrow on whatever sits at the top of that list — every day, until the exam.

The rest of this manual is just the machinery for executing those five laws without friction. Read it once end-to-end, then keep it on your iPad and re-read Page 10 every Sunday.

01Zero to Armed: First-Boot Setup

Ten minutes of setup decides whether the next ten weeks compound or leak.

Step 1 — Run the onboarding wizard honestly

When you first launch SIEGE it walks you through your operator profile. The single most important screen is subject strengths: rate each subject by how you actually perform, not how you wish you did. The whole engine — missions, the mastery radar, the task screener — weights your time toward what you rate weak. Lie here and you'll study what's comfortable instead of what's failing.

Step 2 — Set the clock

  • Exam session & date — e.g. O/N 2026. This drives the Days-Left counter and Burn-Risk maths.
  • Deadline — your internal "ready by" date, ideally 2–3 weeks before the real paper so you have a repair buffer.
  • Daily hours — be realistic; the mission generator sizes each day's plan to this budget.

Step 3 — Connect the brain (Groq API key)

SIEGE's AI runs on Groq, which has a free tier with no credit card. Go to console.groq.com/keys, create a key, paste it into Settings → groq api, and hit save & test until it says groq connected.

04Past-Paper Warfare

The single highest-yield activity in existence. Everything else serves this.

The Past Papers section is a grid of real, indexed CAIE/Edexcel codes. Every card carries direct qp / ms / sme links. Click a card to open the paper intel panel.

The paper-intel modal — your pre-flight check

  • Intel readout — decoded component, marks, time, the real sitting (e.g. "Oct/Nov 2020"), variant/zone, and your status.
  • Open paper — Question Paper / Mark Scheme links that go straight to the real PDF.
  • Log it — set status, enter score/total, and list wrong question numbers + reflection notes. This is what powers your analytics.

✦ Ask AI for a topic

Pick the subject first, then hit ✦ ask ai for topic and name a precise topic ("electrolysis", "sound waves"). SIEGE asks an examiner-model which real papers most likely contain it, validates every code against the actual grid, and filters the grid down to those papers — each flagged with a likely question and reason. Treat results as a strong lead, not gospel: it's an examiner's best estimate, but every paper it shows is real and one click from the PDF.

06Memory & Mistakes: The Compounding Engine

A mistake you log once is worth more than a topic you re-read five times.

Flashcards — the Leitner ladder

Cards live in boxes 1–5. Grade honestly after each: easy jumps the card two boxes, good one, again resets it to box 1. Higher box = longer until you see it again. The discipline is in grading truthfully — a card you "sort of" knew is a good, not an easy.

  • Leech Hunter — auto-suspends cards stuck in box 1. A leech means the card is badly written: break it into smaller atomic facts.
  • Cram Mode — disables spacing and loads the whole deck. Reserve this for the final 48 hours only; it's a last resort, not a method.
  • Build cards from your errors, not from the textbook. Every wrong exam question → one card.

The Mistake Log — your personal weakness map

Log every question you got wrong with its topic. Over weeks this becomes a heat-map of exactly where you bleed marks — and it feeds the Task Screener's yield ranking. Use Redemption Mode to re-attempt your past mistakes; a mistake isn't fixed until you've beaten a similar question cold.

The Notes Vault

Markdown notes per topic, supercharged by AI: Auto-Summarize condenses, Generate Mind Map restructures, Cloze Test turns notes into fill-the-blank active recall, and AI Content Audit cross-checks your notes against the syllabus to flag missing concepts — the gaps you don't know you have.

The compounding lawRe-reading is linear; spaced recall is exponential. Twenty minutes of due-card review every day will, by exam week, have moved more marks than any single all-nighter ever could. Trust the boxes.

Do

  • Grade flashcards honestly
  • Log 100% of wrong questions
  • Review due cards before new study
  • Convert every error into a card

Don't

  • Mark "almost" as easy
  • Skip logging a "silly" mistake
  • Let due cards pile up
  • Cram before the final 48h

07The 12-Week Campaign & Exam-Week Protocol

Coverage early, repair in the middle, simulation at the end. In that order.
PhaseWeeksMission
Foundation1–3Teach every weak topic to a baseline. Lean on CONCEPT TUTOR, EXPLAIN, SOCRATIC. Build your first flashcard decks.
Coverage4–6One real past paper per subject per week. Log everything. Let the radar fill in and the Task Screener wake up.
Saturation7–9Past-paper volume. Timed, Blind Mode on. Use Topic Search to hammer your two weakest radar spokes.
Repair10–11Pure Task-Screener-driven. Each day = the top weakness only, then a paper to prove the fix.
Simulation12Full papers under real exam timing. Curve Estimator after each. Only light, targeted review.

When it's going wrong — Crisis Mode

If the deadline is close and coverage is thin, Burn-Risk will spike. Trigger Crisis Mode and obey the Task Screener ruthlessly: maximum-yield topics only, drop everything already green, accept that "good enough across the board" beats "perfect in one corner."

08The Operator's Twelve Commandments

Pin this page. Re-read it every Sunday. It is the whole manual compressed into twelve lines.
  1. Drill real papers above all else. Nothing else moves marks as fast.
  2. Log every score and every wrong question. Unlogged work is invisible to the engine and, soon, to you.
  3. Let the Task Screener pick your day. Study the top of the yield list, not what's comfortable.
  4. Grade yourself like a hostile examiner. ROAST ME and MARK SCHEME exist to find marks you're losing now, in private, instead of in the hall.
  5. Match the mode to the job. Fifteen specialists beat one generalist.
  6. Obey the command word. Most lost marks are answers to a question that wasn't asked.
  7. Review before you add. Due flashcards first, new study second — always.
  8. Trust the boxes, not the cram. Spaced recall compounds; cramming evaporates.
  9. Watch Burn-Risk weekly. It's the only honest read on whether you're winning the race against the clock.
  10. Protect the streak. Small and logged beats heroic and forgotten.
  11. Fix the method when effort stops yielding. The Effort-vs-Yield chart will tell you when grinding harder is the wrong answer.
  12. Be honest with the terminal. It can only aim you as accurately as you report yourself.
The highest marks on the planet aren't taken in the exam hall. They're assembled, day by logged day, in this terminal — and then simply collected on the day.

Now close the manual, open SIEGE, and build today's mission. The loop is waiting.
